## Listening as Active Practice

Listening is not passive.

It seems passive because you're not producing output. But real listening is intense cognitive work. Tracking context, noticing patterns, building models of what people mean versus what they say. Holding space for ambiguity. Paying attention to what's *not* being said.

Good therapists know this. They don't fill every silence with advice. They let patients sit with discomfort, arrive at insights that mean more because they weren't handed over. The therapist's silence isn't absence of careâ€”it's a different kind of care. Space to think.

## What Silence Says

**Thoughtful pause:** "I'm considering this seriously."

**Respectful distance:** "This is your moment."

**Creative space:** "There's room for something to emerge."

None of these are empty. They're full of meaning. Absence of speech isn't absence of communicationâ€”it's a different modality.
